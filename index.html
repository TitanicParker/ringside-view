A1–A6, from Galway.

An introduction to A1–A6: a method for slowing down a sentence so readers can see what it says, what it governs, and what it rules out.

Ringside View begins in Galway because this is where the work was formed and where I want it first tested.

I went to the Bish. I went to Scoil Caitríona in Renmore. I studied in University College Galway twice. I have worked in Galway City all my life. My life, education, work, friendships, and imagination have all been shaped here.

This project is offered first as a Galway idea: something to be understood, tested, protected, and given a chance to grow.

The method is called A1–A6. It slows a completed declarative sentence down so we can inspect what it does before interpreting it too quickly. It asks what was said, what was governed, what work was done, within what range, what residue remains, and what rival reading is excluded.

The invitation is simple: inspect the method, test the claim, and decide whether it helps you read more carefully.

What A1–A6 makes visible

A1–A6 is not a claim that language can be reduced to a small taxonomy. The public claim is narrower: completed declarative sentences may have recoverable completion-structure. A sentence may carry several pressures, but one Operator becomes dominant at the landing.

The six questions

A1 asks what was said. A2 asks what was governed. A3 asks what work was done. A4 asks within what range. A5 asks what residue remains. A6 asks what rival reading is excluded.

The three axes

From the locked A1–A6 packet, the system recovers Operator, Orientation, and Remainder.

The 54 landings

Six Operators, three Orientations, and three Remainders combine into 54 possible completion-shapes.
