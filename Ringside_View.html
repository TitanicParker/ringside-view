A1–A6 overview

Ringside View.

A public introduction to A1–A6, a method for inspecting what a completed declarative sentence does before interpretation outruns it.

Begin here

A1–A6 slows a completed declarative sentence down so that readers can inspect what the sentence actually does before interpreting it too quickly.

It asks six questions, locks the packet, then recovers three axes: Operator, Orientation, and Remainder. The claim is not that language is being reduced to a small grid. The claim is that completed declarative sentences may have recoverable completion-structure: a dominant kind of work, an attention direction, and a residue mode.

Recovered axes

Operator — Distinction, Expansion, Relation, Correction, Integration, Closure.

Orientation — Toward, Across, Away.

Remainder — Is, With, Does.

Completion-shapes

Six Operators multiplied by three Orientations and three Remainders produce 54 possible landings.

The sentence may carry several pressures, but one Operator becomes dominant at the landing.

What is A1–A6?

A1–A6 is a method for resisting premature interpretation. Instead of jumping straight to what a sentence seems to mean, it asks a reader to form a packet from the sentence’s own completed movement. That packet then allows the three axes to be recovered in a more disciplined way.

The method is designed for slow inspection. It is especially useful where paraphrase, fluent summary, ideological habit, legal assumption, translation pressure, or machine reading might make a sentence appear simpler than it is.

The six questions

A1 — what was saidIdentifies the sentence’s stated content before commentary begins.

A3 — what work was doneNames the sentence-work performed by the completed declaration.
A4 — within what rangeMarks the range in which the work holds.

Uses

A1–A6 is the same method in each setting: slow the sentence down, form the packet, recover the axes, and check what the sentence permits before interpretation extends it. Different readers need it for different reasons.

New readers

Start with one ordinary sentence and see how A1–A6 separates what the sentence says from what a reader might be tempted to import.

Start with the method

Education

Use the six questions to teach students that reading is not only paraphrase or grammar labelling. A sentence says something, governs something, leaves something, and rules something out.

AI research

Use A1–A6 as an anti-overreading protocol before summary, inference, answer generation, or continuation. The packet becomes a check on what the source sentence actually supports.

AI products

Build a sentence-audit layer for tutoring, summarisation checks, legal review, translation support, or model evaluation. The user enters a sentence and receives a disciplined receipt of what it permits and excludes.

Translation

Use A1–A6 to preserve sentence movement across languages, not only surface meaning. A translation should carry what the source sentence governs, leaves, and excludes.

Open multilingual study

Law

Use the packet to inspect clause force: range, residue, and rival readings. The method does not replace legal doctrine; it gives readers a force-map before argument begins.

Linguistics

Test whether the six questions can be recovered inside source-language sentences without silently converting every language into English paraphrase.

Philosophy

Examine whether completed sentences have a recoverable layer between syntax and interpretation: the sentence as a governed event before commentary expands it.

Research validation

Test trained-reader convergence, blind samples, hostile cases, corpus expansion, and inter-reader reliability. The public claim needs pressure, not protection from pressure.
